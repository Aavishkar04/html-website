PART 1: THE NON-NEGOTIABLE RULES

Before the roadmap, these rules come first. Violating any of them is why most people fail.

 

Rule 1: No ChatGPT for Solutions

You can use ChatGPT to explain a concept you don't understand. You cannot use it to write code for you. The moment you copy-paste a solution you didn't understand, you've wasted that hour. Every. Single. Time.

Rule 2: GitHub Commit Every Day

Create a GitHub account this weekend if you don't have one. Every day you write code — even 10 lines — it goes on GitHub. After 3 months your GitHub will be your most powerful resume item. Recruiters look at GitHub streaks.

Rule 3: Struggle Before Searching

When you get stuck, set a timer for 25 minutes. Sit with the problem. Try things. Be wrong. After 25 minutes, then you can look it up — but read the explanation, don't copy the code. This is how understanding is built. There is no shortcut.

Rule 4: Tell Someone Your Goal Tonight

Tell the person at home: 'I am going to get a job paying 80,000 rupees per month by October 2026. Please ask me every Sunday what I built this week.' Accountability is not motivation — it's a structural mechanism.

Rule 5: Start Applying from Month 4, Not When Ready

You will never feel ready. People who wait until they feel ready apply in month 7 and waste 3 months of real interview feedback. Month 4 applications are for practice and learning rejection. Month 5-6 applications are for offers.

Rule 6: One Problem on Leetcode Every Single Day

Starting from Day 1. Easy problems only for the first 8 weeks. Don't skip this even when it takes 2 hours to solve one easy problem. That struggle is exactly the point.

PART 5: THE MENTAL GAME

This section is as important as the technical roadmap. Most people who fail don't fail because of lack of skill. They fail because of what happens in their head.

 

When You Feel Dumb (And You Will)

You told me you feel sad and lose hope when you can't solve basic problems at 23. Here is the reality: every developer alive felt exactly this. The ones who succeeded sat with that feeling and coded anyway. The ones who didn't, stopped. Feeling dumb is not a signal to stop — it is a signal that you are at the edge of what you know, which is precisely where learning happens.

 

When you sit on a problem for an hour and don't solve it: you are not failing. You are building the neural pathways that will let you solve it next time. The frustration is biological — it means your brain is working hard. Welcome it.

 

The 2-Minute Rule for Bad Days

On days when you absolutely cannot face 5 hours: commit to 2 minutes. Open the laptop, open VS Code, write one line of code. Most of the time 2 minutes becomes 30 minutes becomes 2 hours. And on the days it doesn't — you still opened the laptop. The chain is unbroken. The chain is everything.

 

What to Do When You're Stuck for More Than 25 Minutes

•       Step 1: Read the error message out loud. Literally speak it. Often you'll understand it.

•       Step 2: Search the exact error on Google. Stack Overflow will have the answer 90% of the time.

•       Step 3: Read the official documentation for the function/method you're using.

•       Step 4: Ask ChatGPT to explain the concept — not to write the code.

•       Step 5: If still stuck after all of this, write down what you tried, sleep on it, and come back tomorrow. Some problems solve themselves overnight.

 

The Identity Shift

Stop saying 'I am trying to become a developer.' Start saying 'I am a developer who is still learning.' The identity comes first, the skill follows. This sounds like motivational fluff — it is actually neuroscience. The stories we tell about ourselves determine our behaviour.

You asked me to be honest, not supportive. So here it is one last time.

 

You have better conditions than most people who have made this transition. Early schedule, free evenings, free weekends, someone at home, a stable income while you learn, and English fluency. These are real advantages.

 

What you don't have yet is a track record of following through. That is the only thing this plan tests. Not your IQ. Not your math skills. Not whether you feel confident. Just whether the version of you that exists on a random Wednesday at 8pm, tired from work, will open the laptop and write code for 3 hours anyway.

 

If that version of you shows up every day — or almost every day — you will get the job. That's not motivation. That's just how this works.
